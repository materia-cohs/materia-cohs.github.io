Before taking
histories

In obstetric and gynecological practice, intimate details must be elicited; this requires tact, discretion, and maintenance of proper confidentiality.

The usual preparation for history taking should be followed:

  • A courteous greeting and introduction.
  • A statement as to your status. Be open about your status as a medical student or junior doctor.
  • A careful check that you have the correct patient, that she understands the language and seemingly has competence (that she is of appropriate age).
  • In all consultations, you should describe the process that is about to take place and get an agreement or verbal consent.

History must be taken in a non-judgemental, sensitive and thorough manner.

Maintain good communication with the patient in order to elicit proper history and be accurately able to recognize her problems.

Kindness and courtesy must be maintained at all times. A gentle manner and a genuine interest in the patient help the development of a good professional relationship.

It is important for a male doctor to perform examination in the presence of a female chaperone.

Clinicians must adopt both an empathetic and inquisitive attitude towards the patient.

The patientâ€™s privacy must be respected at all costs.

You must refrain from asking personal questions until appropriate patient confidence has been established.

Listen more and talk less while taking history.

Avoid interrupting, commanding and lecturing while taking history.

If any serious condition is suspected, the diagnosis must not be disclosed to the patient until it has been confirmed by performing investigations.

Have several differential diagnoses in mind after each clerking.

Rule out diseases and choose a diagnosis based on the information in the history you took.

Know how you are going to manage the patient for your diagnosis. Management should include things like investigations and treatments.

There are different systems for eliciting a history. We try to make the ones outlined here as comprehensive and relevant as possible by continually updating them as needed and as often as possible.

Not every part of the history taking format outlined is going to be relevant for all patient, hence, it should be adapted to individual patient.
