Obstetric history & exam

History taking is the first step in Doctor-patient relationship.

It serves as a basis for-

  • Physical examination
  • Discussion of investigation modalities
  • Discussion of management modalities

Introduction

Why do we clerk?

To arrive at a diagnosis and plan management.

It is the critical first step in determining the etiology of a problem.

In 70% of cases, diagnosis can be reached from history alone.

We do this by asking questions that are relevant, comprehensive and to the point.

  • Use open-ended questions.
  • Limit leading questions.
  • Remember to enquire about traditional and medical remedies used.
  • Also enquire about life events that may affect patient‚Äôs health.
  • After asking your questions, ask for clarification or summarize history of presenting complaints if necessary.

Tools required in history taking include the ability to-

  • Listen
  • Ask common sense questions
  • An interplay of interpersonal skills, analytical skills and knowledge of medicine
  • A vast knowledge base is required to know the right questions to ask at specific times, this knowledge will be acquired over time.
  • Pregnancy is a physiologic state not a pathologic state.
  • Patients are usually healthy.
  • Two patients in one- mother and fetus.
  • Balance maternal and fetal outcome.
  • The aim of antenatal clinic is to have a healthy infant and mother at the end of pregnancy.

Approach to history taking

Dress smartly and professionally. First impressions last longest.

Be confident.

Greet patient and establish a good rapport.

Introduce yourself in a friendly manner, explain what you are about to do e.g. I am a medical student and I would like to ask you a few questions about yourself and your pregnancy. Is that OK? Or can I go ahead?

‚ÄòHer story‚Äô the history should be reported as much as possible in patient's own words.

Be understanding, non-judgmental, receptive, use verbal and non-verbal cues (eye-contact, nodding, etc).

Demonstrate sensitivity to religious and traditional beliefs.

Try to be efficient and time conscious; focus on main problem and don't feel compelled to ask every single question on the list.

Remember to document date and time, name/signature at the end.

Demographic data

Biodata

Name- this can give an indication of the person‚Äôs tribe and cultural beliefs.

Age- this can help anticipate possible complications e.g. in a teenager or an elderly primigravida.

Occupation- this can give information about possible exposure to toxins/chemicals that may later be required for diagnosis and treatment.

Religion- each religion has peculiar beliefs that may influence management and they should be respected at the request of the patient. The Jehovah Witnesses for example do not take blood transfusions.

Marital status- this may provide information about whether or not the woman will have adequate social support and also whether pregnancy is desired.

Level of education- this can how you communicate effectively with a patient and in what language. You might need to speak in a native dialect to some uneducated patient.

Address- this may provide information about proximity to healthcare facilities in case of emergencies.

Gravidity & Parity

Reported as (GxPy+z, a alive)

G= gravida

P= para

x= gravidity, total number of pregnancies irrespective of site, duration or outcome i.e. number of times she has been pregnant before.

y= parity, total number of previous deliveries irrespective of outcome (i.e. total pregnancies carried up to the age of viability which is 28 weeks in this environment). Twin delivery is considered a single parous experience (i.e. para 1).

z= number of pregnancies not carried to the age of viability (e.g. spontaneous or induced miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, molar pregnancy)

a= number of children alive

note

Obstetric terminologies

  • Nullipara : A woman who has never given birth.
  • Nulligravida: A woman who has never been pregnant.
  • Primigravida: A woman who is pregnant for the first time.
  • Primipara: A woman who has had her first delivery.
  • Multipara: A woman who has had more than 1 previous deliveries.
  • Grand-multipara: A woman who has had 5 or more deliveries.

Last menstrual period

Very fundamental. It is used to calculate an estimated delivery/due date (LMP) and estimated gestational age (EGA).

Make sure to ask for the first day of the last menstrual period, the patient might think you are asking for the last day they saw their period.

If unsure, relate to events, quickening (occurs between 16 ‚Äì 18 weeks in a multigravida and 18 ‚Äì 20 weeks in a primigravida) or ask about ultrasound scans to obtain the estimated gestational age (EGA).

Estimated due/delivery date (EDD)

It is based on an average pregnancy length of 280 day (40 weeks) from the first day of the LMP (Naegele‚Äôs rule).

  • 4% of women deliver on EDD
  • 6 ‚Äì 7% at < 37 weeks
  • 4 ‚Äì 14% at > 42 weeks
  • others between 37 ‚Äì 40 weeks.

Estimated gestational age (EGA)

Must be accurately calculated to ensure appropriate management. It also serves as the basis for determining whether a baby is term, preterm or post-term.

It serves as the basis for determining whether a baby is term, preterm or post-term.

A pre-term baby is born before EGA of 37 weeks.

A term baby is born between EGA of 37 weeks and 42 weeks.

A post-term baby is born after EGA of 42 weeks.

Presenting complaint

Get the chief complain using open-ended questions.

What is bothering you right now?

What brings you to the hospital today?

How can I help you?

Clarify if needed. ‚ÄòWhat are you feeling right now?‚Äô

Survey for other problems. ‚ÄòWhat else?‚Äô

Always remember to focus on the most important problems first.

Allow her to describe the problem in her own words.

Allow her time to answer, try not to interrupt.

May be several; arrange in order of occurrence.

State the timing of each occurrence.

For example,

  • 8 months amenorrhea (LMP, EDD, EGA).
  • Bleeding per vaginam of 2 hours duration.

History of present complaint

Explore each symptom individually if more than one complain.

Mention important positives and significant negatives e.g. not sexually active in a reproductive age woman with acute abdomen.

Avoid medical terminology as much as possible.

Have relevant differential diagnosis in mind.

Remember the 5 Cs

  • Chief complain/presenting complain
  • Course
  • Cause
  • Complication
  • Care so far

Associated symptoms

Fever

Tiredness/fatigue

Pain- this can be explained using the acronym SOCRATES.

  • Site- where exactly is the pain
  • Onset- when did it begin? Was it sudden or gradual?
  • Character- sharp, continuous, dull, aching, burning
  • Radiation- does it move somewhere else?
  • Associations- any associated symptoms?
  • Time course- does it have a pattern e.g. worse at certain periods.
  • Exacerbating/relieving factors- what makes it worse or better?
  • Severity ‚Äì on a scale of 0 to 10, how much pain are you feeling (0 being no pain and 10 being the worst pain imaginable).

Systemic review

Screen for symptoms in each body system that have not already been discussed; this helps as a guide to make sure that no symptom is missed out?

From head to toe:

  • Skin: any skin problems? Rash? Itch? Sores?
  • Eyes: eye problems? Vision loss? Blurry vision? Double vision?
  • Ears, nose, mouth, sinuses, and throat: any trouble with hearing, ears, mouth, and throat?
  • Lungs: any breathing problems? Coughs? Chest pain? Sputum?
  • Heart: heart problems? Palpitations? Skipping beats? Chest pain?
  • Digestive: appetite, stomach pain? Nausea? Vomiting? Diarrhea? Constipation? Bloating? Weight loss?
  • Genitourinary: frequency, urgency, dysuria, polyuria, vaginal discharge
  • Endocrine: feeling too cold or too hot compared to others? Excessive thirst, hunger, or urination?
  • Musculoskeletal: muscle/joint pain, swelling, weakness?
  • Neurological: weakness, numbness, or tingling in arms or legs? Seizures?
  • Psychiatric: anxiety? Depression? Suicidal or homicidal urges?
