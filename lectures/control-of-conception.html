Control of Conception (Family Planning)

Introduction

Family planning programs have yielded dramatically positive gains over the past 50 years.

However, there are still 123 million women around the world, mostly in developing countries, who are not using contraception in spite of an expressed desire to space or limit the number of births.

Contraceptive use is still low and the need high in some of the world‚Äôs poorest and most populous places.

Contraception has been practiced by the human race for thousands of years.

Organized family planning and modern contraceptives started around the late 1950‚Äôs-1960

The need for limiting population by virtue of family size is a global emergency.

Many women die each year from complications of pregnancy, childbirth and induced abortions.

Effective contraception can reduce maternal morbidity, mortality as well as population explosion and its consequences.

An Ideal contraceptive agent should be

  • Safe
  • Effective
  • Convenient
  • Independent of coitus
  • Initiated by single simple procedure
  • Reversible
  • Cheap
  • Socially and culturally acceptable and requiring no or minimal medical supervision

WHO has published the medical eligibility criteria to classify health conditions into four categories for initiation and continuation of each contraceptive, taking into account the health risk and benefits of using such method for a woman with given conditions.

  • Category 1: Always usable, No restriction on use.
  • Category 2: Broadly usable, advantages overweigh risks.
  • Category 3: Use with caution, risks outweigh advantages.
  • Category 4: Do not use, has unacceptable health risks.

Family Planning in Nigeria

In the past three decades, Nigeria has made very bold efforts to achieve rapid economic development.

However, amongst other factors, rapid population growth has affected the quality of life and made achievement of socio-economic development goals difficult.

Key findings from 2003 NDHS:

Only 13% of married women use any method of family planning.

Although three-quarters of women know of at least one method of modern family planning, only 8 percent of currently married women are using one.

Contraceptive knowledge and use vary significantly by region.

There are many missed opportunities for increasing family planning acceptance and use.

Seventeen (17) percent of currently married women have an unmet need for family planning.

Types of modern Family Planning methods available in Nigeria

  • Hormonal Methods
  • Intra-uterine Contraceptive Devices
  • Barrier Methods
  • Natural Family Planning
  • Voluntary Surgical Contraception
  • Immunological Methods
  • Male Methods

Hormonal methods:

Oral Pills

  • Combined pills
  • Progestin-only pills

Vaginal Pills

  • Combined pills
    • Intermittent
    • Continuous

Injectables

  • Progestin-only Injectables
    • Depot Medroxyprogesterone acetate (DMPA)
    • Norethisterone enanthate (NET-EN)
  • Combined Injectables
    • Cyclofem
    • Mesigyna

Implants

  • Non-Biodegradable Implants
    • Norplant/Jadelle
    • Uniplant
    • Implanon
  • Biodegradable Implants
    • Annuelle

Intra-uterine contraceptive devices (IUCD):

Non-medicated (or first generation) IUCDs e.g., Lippes loop, Dalkon Shield.

Medicated (or second generation) IUCDs

  • Serve as carriers or vehicles for pharmacologically active anti-fertility agents e.g., Copper T380A, Multiload (MLCu-375), Nova-T (which contains Cu and silver); Progestasert (which elaborates progesterone but discontinued).

Barrier methods:

Oldest means of contraception.

  • Male Condoms
  • Female Condoms
  • Diaphragms
  • Cervical caps
  • Vaginal sponges
    • They also usually contain spermicides.

Natural Family Planning methods:

Lactational Amenorrhoea Method (LAM).

Cervical Mucus or Billings Method.

Ovulation Method.

Calendar (Rhythm Method).

Sympto-thermal Method.

Voluntary surgical contraception (VSC):

Bilateral Tubal Occlusion

  • Tubal Ligation
  • Electrocoagulation
  • Use of Clips (Filschie) and Rings (Falope)
  • Use of Chemicals

Vasectomy

  • Conventional vasectomy
  • No Scalpel vasectomy

Immunological Methods/ Male Methods

  • Contraceptive Vaccines
    • Anti-HCG Vaccine
    • LH/RH Vaccine
  • Male Methods
    • Gossypol
