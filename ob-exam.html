Obstetric
Examination

Preparation
  • If male examiner, request for a chaperone to be present.
  • Wash hands, wear your gloves.
  • Explain the steps of the examination to the patient as this reduces anxiety and enhances cooperation
  • Obtain consent
  • The patient should be advised to void as an empty bladder promotes comfort and allows for more productive examination.
  • Provision of privacy
  • Prepare the equipment- measuring tape, Pinnard stethoscope or doppler transducer, ultrasound gel, paper towels.
  • Position the patient
  • She can be rolled slightly to herleft to prevent compression of large vessels- aorta/inferior vena cava (inferior vena caval syndrome or supine hypotensive syndrome.)
  • Adequate exposure of the gravid abdomen from the xiphisternum to the pubic symphysis
  • Also expose legs up to the knee.
General physical examination

General observation/appearance

  • Is the patient in any apparent distress?
  • Is she drowsy/unconscious; not oriented to time, place and person?
  • Lying/sitting comfortably (with IV cannula in rt. /lt. hand/Foleyâ€™s catheter in situ)

Body mass index (BMI)

  • Height
  • Weight
  • BMI (Weight in kg/height2 in m2) - pregnancy complications are increased with a BMI <18.5 and >25

Eye

  • Conjuctival pallor for anemia
  • Scleral jaundice

Mouth

  • Mouth hygiene, caries, and denture
  • Dorsum of the tongue- dehydration, leukoplakia
  • Tip of the tongue- central cyanosis
  • Ventral surface of the tongue- buccal mucosa pallor
  • Buccal mucosa for cyanosis and dehydration

Neck

  • Inspect the neck from the front for any obvious swelling. If there is a swelling, perform a proper thyroid examination.
  • Lymph node enlargement(cervical and supraclavicular LN).

Hand

  • Palmar crease for pallor
  • Capillary refill
  • Finger clubbing
  • Repeat on contralateral hand

Pedal edema

  • Posterior aspect of the lateral malleolus.
  • Press for 15 seconds.
  • Check for dimpling. If it dimples, move to the tibia, then knee, then sacrum.

Varicose veins and skeletal abnormalities.

Use PILCCED for easy recall when recording- Pallor, Icterus, Lymph nodes, Cyanosis, Clubbing, Edema, Dehydration status.

Abdominal examination

Inspection

  • Expose the patients up to the pubic hairline inferiorly.
  • Patientâ€™s cloth should go below the breasts superiorly.
  • Note the apparent size of the abdomen and distension.
  • Check for symmetry.
  • Movement with respiration.
  • Cutaneous signs of pregnancy-
    • Linea nigra- dark pigmented line stretching from the xiphisternum through the umbilicus to the suprapubic area. It is due to increased melanocyte stimulating hormone made by the placenta.
    • Striae gravidarum- recent stretch marks, purplish? in color. Striae is due to increased cortisol.
    • Striae albicans- old stretch marks, silvery-white.
  • Umbilicus- flat or everted.
  • Scarification marks.
  • Surgical scars- a low pfannenstiel incision may be obscured by pubic hair, and laparoscopic scars hidden within the umbilicus.
  • Superficial veins- alternate paths of venous drainage due to pressure on the IVC by gravid uterus.

Palpation

Superficial palpation:

  • Ask the patient if they feel pain anywhere in the abdomen. The region of pain should be palpated last.
  • Palpate all 9 regions of the abdomen.
  • For tenderness- generalized or localized.
  • Always observe the patientâ€™s face for signs of discomfort.

Deep palpation:

  • for organomegaly
    • Hepatomegaly- start from the right iliac fossa and move upwards.
    • Splenomegaly- in pregnant women, start from the left iliac fossa and move upwards.
  • Uterine/ovarian mass (site, size, shape, surface, tenderness, consistency, mobility, edges, attachment).
Uterus & it's content palpation

Symphysio-fundal height (SFH) estimation

SFH answers 2 questions-

  1. Normality of the pregnancy
  2. Rate of growth of the pregnancy

Method:

  • Locate the fundus starting from the xiphisternum using the ulna border of the left hand until resistance is encountered, place the tape under the ulna border of the hand with the inch side facing the examiner and leave the tape.
  • Locate the upper border of the symphysis pubis in the midline using the index finger of the right hand.
  • Take the measurement with the inch side and turn to read in cm.
  • Report example- SFH is 26cm which is consistent with 26wks of intrauterine pregnancy and compatible with the EGA.
  • If SFH is greater or less than 2 weeks EGA, then it is incompatible.

Greater than 2 weeks differences:

  • Wrong dates
  • Multiple pregnancy
  • Polyhydraminios
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Fibroids in pregnancy
  • Hydatiform
  • Fetal abnormalities e.g. anencephaly
  • Familial
  • Obesity

Less than 2 weeks differences:

  • Wrong date
  • Intrauterine growth restriction
  • Oligohydramnios
  • Congenital anomaly

Fundal height estimation using anatomic landmarks:

  • Level of symphysis pubis- 12 weeks
  • Level at the lower margin of the umbilicus- 20 weeks
  • Level of umbilicus- 22 weeks
  • Level of the xiphisternum- 36 weeks
  • Fundal heights at any point below or above the landmarks is assessed by adding the finger breadth to the immediate landmark. A standard finger breadth is assumed to be 2cm = 2weeks.

Example:

The SFH 32cm which corresponds to an intrauterine gestation of 32 weeks plus or minus 2 weeks and is compatible with/less/greater than her EGA of xx today.

Leopoldâ€™s maneuvers

The Leopoldâ€™s maneuvers are used to palpate the gravid uterus systematically.

The maneuvers can help to determine the-

  • Fetal pole
  • Fetal lie
  • Fetal presentation
  • Fetal engagement

It is important to know that all findings are not truly diagnostic, and as such ultrasound may be required to conclusively determine these parameters.

The maneuvers are difficult to perform on obese women and women who have polyhydraminios.

The first maneuver/The fundal grip:

Purpose: To determine the fetal part lying in the fundus. To determine presentation

Procedure: Using both hands, feel for the fetal part lying in the fundus.

Findings:

  • The fetal buttocks is broad, soft, with an even surface and is non-ballotable.
  • The fetal head is hard and round with a smooth surface and of uniform consistency, is very mobile and ballotable.
  • The buttocks normally occupies the upper pole while the head occupies the lower pole.

The second maneuver/The lateral grip:

Purpose: To identify the location of the fetal back. To determine fetal lie.

Procedure: One hand is used to steady the uterus on one side of the abdomen while the other hand moves slightly on a circular motion from the top to the lower segment of the uterus to feel for the fetal back and small fetal parts. Use gentle but deep pressure.

The fetal lie is the relationship between the longitudinal axis of the fetus and the longitudinal axis of the mother. The â€˜lieâ€™ is usually longitudinal, i.e. the baby is lying in the same longitudinal axis as the mother. Other â€˜liesâ€™ are transverse and oblique lies.

Findings:

  • The fetal back is smooth, hard, and resistant surface.
  • The fetal knees and elbows feels irregular and lumpy.

The third maneuver/Pawlikâ€™s Grip:

Purpose: To determine the presenting part and its engagement. The presenting part of the fetus is the lowermost part of the fetus at the inlet of the pelvis.

Procedure: The differentiation between the head and breech is made as in the first maneuver. This maneuver should validate the findings of the first maneuver.

Using the thumb and other fingers, grasp the lower portion of the abdomen above symphisis pubis, press in slightly and make gentle movements from side to side to determine engagement of the presenting part.

Findings: If the presenting part moves, then it is not engaged.

The fourth maneuver:

Purpose: to determine the attitude/degree of flexion of the fetus.

Procedure: Resembles the first maneuver; however, the examiner faces towards the maternal pelvis. The fingers of both hands moves gently along the sides of the uterus towards the pubis. Palpate for the cephalic prominence (vertex).

Findings: Prominence on the same side as the small parts suggests that the head is flexed (normal). Prominence on the same side as the back suggests that the head is extended.

Additional uterine assessment

The myometrium/uterine wall:

  • Comment on whether the myometrium is soft (normal antenatal state) or contracting (normal state when in labor is 30-60 sec period of being firm to hard followed by a 2-5 min interval of being soft).
  • It may also be hard in abruption placenta.

The liquor volume:

  • Assessment made of the volume of amniotic fluid surrounding the fetus.
  • Reduced volume is called Oligohydramnios, the fetal parts are easily felt.
  • Increased volume is called polyhydramnios, there is difficulty feeling the fetal parts.

Estimation of fetal weight:

  • Difficult and requires practice.
  • Fetus at 28 weeks gestation and SFH of 28cm is approximately 1.1kg.
  • A 34 week fetus with SFH of 34cm is approximately 2.2kg.
  • A term fetus (40 weeks) with a variable SFH between 36cm and 40cm is approximately 3.3kg.
  • Each week between these parameters accounts for about 200g.

Fetal movements:

  • During the examination, note any fetal movements (kicks and rolling motions).

Fetal heart rate:

  • The fetal heart should be auscultated using a fetal stethoscope.
    • A Pinnard stethoscope (a wood, metal or plastic device) or
    • An ultrasonic fetal doppler (an electronic device)

  • Thank the patient for their time.
  • Dispose of PPE appropriately and wash your hands.
